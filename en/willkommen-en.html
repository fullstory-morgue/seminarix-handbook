Seminarix is a Live-medium based on sidux, which is developed for the education of teachers.

Welcome to the project "Seminarix"

On the following pages you will find documentation which is intended to provide in-depth information regarding the project "Seminarix - the live-medium for the education of teachers".

The project's background is the following:

The discussion about "closed" and "open" software, particulary the open source movement, and its role in media-critical education, has reached the schools.

The debate about closed/open standards needs urgent political discussion about software and its availability on one hand, and on the other hand the economical benefits of open source software versus closed source software, (to finance monopolies is and will remain expensive!).

The Seminarix project

The idea arose, as more and more live evaluation CDs with the Linux operating system appeared, which allowed a first look at the open and very transparently configurable Linux (in its manifold manifestations) on numerous and also very different computer systems.

A deficiency that quickly became evident, was that software which was (or wasn't) of significance for the teacher's training, had to be laboriously installed.

And it should be, at this point, honestly admitted, that this was, until some time ago in some distributions not an undertaking that a newcomer could "just" carry out (nor should).

So was born the idea: Why not create a live-CD or maybe even a live-DVD which will contain as much educational software as possible, to demonstrate the possibilities of an open source system.

The choice fell on the Debian distribution family. For one, it can be very easily kept up to date and on the other hand, it contains a very smart routine to install new software and to uninstall software which is no longer needed.

A critical reason to choose Debian is Debian and its social contract (DFSG). The main principles are presented here:

  • Everyone has the right to use, without limits, all the computer programs of his choice.
  • Everyone has the right to copy and distribute, arbitrary many times, his programs of choice without having to pay for them.
  • Everyone has the right to, without limits, edit the programs so they meet his wishes/requirements.
  • All feel obliged to share with everyone their experience, their knowledge, questions, problems and solutions and with that to build a community that is ready to help.

It is understandable that the development and implementation, collection and testing of the software and its installation/integration is not as easy as it reads here. Therefore in the beginning the first choice was the very tidy-looking Kubuntu (6.06). This version was published in April 2007. In the autumn of 2007, discussions with the sidux team indicated that with sidux a faster basis for Seminarix could be found. This is now also history, sidux-Seminarix was first appeared at the LinuxTag 2008 in Berlin. Now we want to release the first Live-DVD.

Accompanying information

The information found here should inform you about which applications are contained on the live-Medium. Sources (for software, documentation and other information, sometimes including wikis) are also mentioned, as are instructions on how to deal with the respective software.

Should we have tips and suggestions for a specific program, which might be of interest to others, we will post it on http://www.seminarix.org.

On the idea of free software is is also imperative that, in addition to the sources from which the applications was built, the documentation be included on the medium from which the system will be installed.

This was for this live-CD, because of space limitations, not possible. Many applications would not have had space on the CD. By using a DVD, a big part of the documentation will be installed.

If the documentation is not on the live-medium under /usr/share/docs, you can find links to the project web-pages under the text describing each program.

You can find support from and for this project in a variety of ways.

For one we hope that the live-medium, and of course the project's website, has already provided you with a lot of sound suggestions and guidelines.

Secondly there are a lot of manuals, short guides and online help on the Internet. Space was not the only reason we have refrained from including all this help in the live-Medium. You can not install programs on to a CD, with a DVD you can do much more. You can find this help under the given links for each application.

Additional hint: If you want the results of your work to persist when you are using a live-system, then you have to use some portable storage (eg USB).

You can find this media according to their respective recognition process in the directory tree under / media.

Remember: Everything that was stored in the ram by the live-medium will be, after you shutdown the computer, irrecoverably erased!

And: Do not forget: A double click is actually a click too much. In Linux, applications start with a single click.

Final Words

We hope that this live-medium and the information associated with it, contribute to bringing the philosophy behind free software and open standards to an even bigger and more interested audience so it can become known and discussed.

From our point of view, a serious discussion about monopolies and the question if operating systems and software are goods which should be publicly available, is part of a critical debate about (digital) media.

This project, in conjunction with other projects, has the goal to provide the educational sector with an able and viable alternative.

Coorperation with the following projects have been established.

The "Skolelinux" project: Its objective is to bring to schools a Debian based Linux-solution which can, with little administrative effort, provide schools with well functioning Linux computers for the student's everyday work.

A big developers "family" works voluntarily on it (same goes for sidux and other linux projects) so students and teachers can use it (contributors are welcome).

A further project that has to be specifically mentioned here is the "KDE-EDU" project. This project, as you can guess from its name, tries to provide KDE (a very popular Linux desktop) with educational programs.

There are already numerous applications which are part of that project and which are suitable for usage in schools. You can recognize these projects from the K they usually have at the beginning of their name. You can find many of those on the live-medium.
